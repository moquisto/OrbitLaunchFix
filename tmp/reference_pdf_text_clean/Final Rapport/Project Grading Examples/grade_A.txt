2 Problem
Using the model and Monte Carlo method described, we will investigate the behavior of neutron trans-
mission through the slab when varying the diﬀerent physical parameters, ps, λ, d, involved. We will
attempt to answer the question: How does the attenuation of the neutron transmission intensity change
when varying the physical involved?
We will also investigate the change in the transport process when changing the absorption capabilities
of the bulk material. In particular, we will examine how a uniform mix (material atoms uniformly
randomly distributed in the bulk) between two materials when varying the proportions of the two material
and compare with the case of a pure, homogeneous slab. We will attempt to answer the question: Could
one increase the shielding capabilities of a slab better by proportions of a better shielding material, better
than increase the depth of the slab?

3.2 Attenuation
First we perform a test to see how well our model correspond to known behavior of neutron radiation,
by measuring the attenuation of the transmitted neutron intensity (corresponding to the transmission
probabilitypt) as a function of neutron energy and depth of the isolating bulk.
The Beer-Lambert law of exponential attenuation is known to hold for neutron radiation, so we should
expect exponential reduction of transmitted intensity when increasing bulk depth.
The increase of neutron energy in our model corresponds to an increase in the mean free path λ. Also,
since an increase in λ is the same as reducing the average number of nuclear interactions needed before
being transmitted, one can suspect that increasing λ would have the same eﬀect as reducing the depth
d. We therefore also examine the transmission intensity as a function of λ and the possible similarity to
the dependence on d.
3.3 Material inhomogeneity
We examine the dependence of event probabilities on the material properties of scattering and absorption
probabilities. In an attempt to answer how material impurities can aﬀect the system, we let the bulk be
a mix between materials of varying absorption capabilities, and vary the proportion between materials.
We compare the probabilities obtained from mixing two materials with the ones obtained from letting
the bulk consist entirely of one material.

5 Discussion and Conclusions
In the Attenuation section, we the ﬁtted exponential function in ﬁg. 1 does not deviate from the data
in any aspect measurable in the ﬁgure and is enclosed by the error bounds of the data points. We can
therefore conclude that for the parameter conﬁguration tested, the attenuation follows Beer-Lambert’s
law, being exponentially decreasing with slab depth.
As we further tested the behavior when instead varying the mean free path of the neutrons, the
ﬁtted exponential function aeb/x ﬁt the data just as well as in the last case, with measurements having a
negligible error of less than 0.01 for allλ tested. When varying ratio instead of each parameter separately
in ﬁg. 3, we had errors just as small as the ones measured in the previous, and there too an exponential
function ﬁt to the data, with no mentionable deviation from the data. Considering the small errors
between ﬁtted function and data points, we can safely conclude that the exponential attenuation holds
for this model for the investigated parameter conﬁgurations, as it does with real neutron transmission.
This, despite neglecting not obviously small contributions such as ﬁssion during nuclear interaction.
Performing the convergence test in ﬁg. 5 and examining the measured error during the tests, such as
in ﬁg. 4, we see that the error varies with the measured result. The error seems maximum for results
measuring p≈ 0.5 and is reduced as the distance to either p = 0 or p = 1 is. This is likely an eﬀect
of averaging results of a discrete stochastic variable only taking values of 0 and 1. As the values of the
mean are bounded below by 0 and above 1, the variance can be predicted to be reduced for mean values
measured close to 0 or 1. In the p≈ 0.5 case, the mean values can vary maximally in both directions,
causing a greater variance to be measured.
Further examining the behavior of the error estimate at low probability results, we see that while the
error is reduced, the number of Monte Carlo samples W needed to converge toward the reduction trend
is larger than for the other measurements. Both this and the reduced error for these measurements could
come from the fact that we have a certain uncertainty in our result not completely encapsulated by the
error estimation done according to (10). Since we are measuring probabilities as fractions of scores kept

by repeated simulation, in order to measure a probability of order 10 −k, one would need a number of
measurementsW of the order 10k or more. Thus it is not odd that the estimated error ﬂuctuates more
for p≈ 0.01 when we are only using between W = 10 2 and W = 10 3 measurements. It is important
to keep this in mind when using this kind of Monte Carlo simulation, relying on evaluation of discrete
stochastic variables.
But all in all, judging from the convergence plot in ﬁg. 5, the error seems to decrease asymptotically
accordingO(W −1/2), and all measurements reach a satisfactory and probable error of less than 10 −2 for
W = 105, which was used in the rest of our measurements.
After taking this error analysis into consideration, the continued investigation of attenuation, now
analyzing the eﬀect of the scattering probability ps showed in ﬁg. 6 and table 2 that decreasing ps
changes the exponent. Fitted exponential functions still had extremely good agreement with obtained
data, with the only case standing out was for ps = 0.99 with a goodness of ﬁt measure R2≈ 0.98. This
shows that the exponential attenuation law shown to hold for one parameter conﬁguration, also hold when
changing other physical parameters. To answer the question we stated in the problem statement: The
attenuation of transmission intensity during neutron transport through a slab is exponential in the ratio
of depth and mean free path, with the rate of intensity decay determined by the scattering probability
of the material.
Regarding the absorption probabilitypc as a function of the scattering probability of the slab, creating
a mixture of two materials seems to create a material with average absorbing capabilities being the mean
values of the materials, weighted by the respective fraction of material atoms, as indicated by the linear
trend in ﬁg. 8. This means that by adding better absorbents to the slab, it would increase the material’s
overall absorbing ability, but one would achieve a greater improvement by replacing the entire slab by
a slightly better absorbent. Since increasing the depth also would increase absorptions considerably (as
pt decreases exponentially and total probability is conserved pt +pc +pr = 1), creating mixed materials
would probably not compare. The accuracy of this result though, is debatable, since it is based on the
mixing of materials being uniform. The considerable simpliﬁcation in our model of randomly picking
the interacting material would not hold for slightly non-uniform material distributions and also does not
account for neutrons in the simulations arriving in the same location twice. One should probably have
to add certain details to the model such as deﬁning intervals for the nuclei positions if keeping a discrete
model is desirable. Although this would also mean a large amount of memory overhead for any decent
accuracy, and is thus not attempted here.
