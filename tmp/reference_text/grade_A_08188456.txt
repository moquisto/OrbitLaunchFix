5 Discussion and Conclusions
In the Attenuation section, we the fitted exponential function in fig. 1 does not deviate from the data
in any aspect measurable in the figure and is enclosed by the error bounds of the data points. We can
therefore conclude that for the parameter configuration tested, the attenuation follows Beer-Lambert’s
law, being exponentially decreasing with slab depth.
As we further tested the behavior when instead varying the mean free path of the neutrons, the
fitted exponential function aeb/x fit the data just as well as in the last case, with measurements having a
negligibleerroroflessthan0.01forallλtested. Whenvaryingratioinsteadofeachparameterseparately
in fig. 3, we had errors just as small as the ones measured in the previous, and there too an exponential
function fit to the data, with no mentionable deviation from the data. Considering the small errors
between fitted function and data points, we can safely conclude that the exponential attenuation holds
for this model for the investigated parameter configurations, as it does with real neutron transmission.
This, despite neglecting not obviously small contributions such as fission during nuclear interaction.
Performing the convergence test in fig. 5 and examining the measured error during the tests, such as
in fig. 4, we see that the error varies with the measured result. The error seems maximum for results
measuring p ≈ 0.5 and is reduced as the distance to either p = 0 or p = 1 is. This is likely an effect
of averaging results of a discrete stochastic variable only taking values of 0 and 1. As the values of the
mean are bounded below by 0 and above 1, the variance can be predicted to be reduced for mean values
measured close to 0 or 1. In the p ≈ 0.5 case, the mean values can vary maximally in both directions,
causing a greater variance to be measured.
Further examining the behavior of the error estimate at low probability results, we see that while the
error is reduced, the number of Monte Carlo samples W needed to converge toward the reduction trend
islargerthanfortheothermeasurements. Boththisandthereducederrorforthesemeasurementscould
come from the fact that we have a certain uncertainty in our result not completely encapsulated by the
error estimation done according to (10). Since we are measuring probabilities as fractions of scores kept

by repeated simulation, in order to measure a probability of order 10−k, one would need a number of
measurements W of the order 10k or more. Thus it is not odd that the estimated error fluctuates more
for p ≈ 0.01 when we are only using between W = 102 and W = 103 measurements. It is important
to keep this in mind when using this kind of Monte Carlo simulation, relying on evaluation of discrete
stochastic variables.
But all in all, judging from the convergence plot in fig. 5, the error seems to decrease asymptotically
according O(W−1/2), and all measurements reach a satisfactory and probable error of less than 10−2 for
W =105, which was used in the rest of our measurements.
After taking this error analysis into consideration, the continued investigation of attenuation, now
analyzing the effect of the scattering probability p showed in fig. 6 and table 2 that decreasing p
s s
changes the exponent. Fitted exponential functions still had extremely good agreement with obtained
data, with the only case standing out was for p =0.99 with a goodness of fit measure R2 ≈0.98. This
s
showsthattheexponentialattenuationlawshowntoholdforoneparameterconfiguration,alsoholdwhen
changing other physical parameters. To answer the question we stated in the problem statement: The
attenuation of transmission intensity during neutron transport through a slab is exponential in the ratio
of depth and mean free path, with the rate of intensity decay determined by the scattering probability
of the material.
Regardingtheabsorptionprobabilityp asafunctionofthescatteringprobabilityoftheslab,creating
c
amixtureoftwomaterialsseemstocreateamaterialwithaverageabsorbingcapabilitiesbeingthemean
values of the materials, weighted by the respective fraction of material atoms, as indicated by the linear
trend in fig. 8. This means that by adding better absorbents to the slab, it would increase the material’s
overall absorbing ability, but one would achieve a greater improvement by replacing the entire slab by
a slightly better absorbent. Since increasing the depth also would increase absorptions considerably (as
p decreases exponentially and total probability is conserved p +p +p =1), creating mixed materials
t t c r
would probably not compare. The accuracy of this result though, is debatable, since it is based on the
mixing of materials being uniform. The considerable simplification in our model of randomly picking
the interacting material would not hold for slightly non-uniform material distributions and also does not
account for neutrons in the simulations arriving in the same location twice. One should probably have
to add certain details to the model such as defining intervals for the nuclei positions if keeping a discrete
model is desirable. Although this would also mean a large amount of memory overhead for any decent
accuracy, and is thus not attempted here.
